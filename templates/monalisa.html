Mona Lisa

Painting by Leonardo da Vinci

About the Painting

Artist: Leonardo da Vinci

Year of Creation: 1503–1506 (possibly until 1517)

Medium: Oil on wood

Dimensions: 77 x 53 cm

Location: Louvre Museum, Paris

Description: "Mona Lisa" is a portrait of a woman with a mysterious smile, which has become an icon of world art. The painting is executed in the sfumato technique, creating smooth transitions between light and shadow. It is an example of Leonardo da Vinci's outstanding craftsmanship, his ability to convey human emotions and psychological states through art.

Context and Influence on Art

"Mona Lisa" is not just a painting, but a masterpiece that changed the perception of portraits and how people are represented in art. The use of sfumato to create soft transitions between light and shadow was revolutionary at the time. The painting also became a symbol of the evolution of portraiture, emphasizing the psychological depth of the character rather than just their appearance.

The smile of Mona Lisa, which has become the subject of numerous interpretations, remains one of the most mysterious features of the painting. It has been the topic of philosophical reflections, scientific studies, and, of course, a source of inspiration for many artists, photographers, and other creative individuals.

The influence of the painting is still felt in art through the centuries. Artists like Salvador Dalí used "Mona Lisa" as a motif in their works. Its influence on pop art is also immense, as the painting became a cultural phenomenon.

The History of the Mona Lisa

The Mona Lisa, painted by Leonardo da Vinci between 1503 and 1506, is one of the most famous artworks in the world. Originally created in Florence, the painting later traveled to France, where it has been housed in the Louvre Museum since the French Revolution. The painting's mysterious smile and Leonardo's innovative techniques, such as the use of sfumato, have made it an enduring symbol of art and culture.
